Anti-Corruption Index (IPAK) by Regions 2012–2024

The Anti-Corruption Behavior Index (IPAK) measures public behavior and attitudes toward corruption in both urban and rural areas. This visualization captures Indonesia’s evolving awareness and integrity culture over a 12-year period.

General Analysis: 2012–2024

Increased National Awareness — Both urban and rural regions show an upward trend in anti-corruption behavior, indicating growing integrity and transparency across Indonesia.

Challenges in Rural Areas — While improvements are visible after 2020, rural areas still experience higher volatility, suggesting disparities in enforcement and civic engagement.

Policy Implications — These patterns reflect ongoing governmental efforts but also highlight the need for stronger outreach in local communities.

Indonesian Anti-Corruption Behaviors by Ages (2014–2024)

Each line represents a different age group, illustrating how the anti-corruption index evolved over the years for each generation. The visual highlights behavioral trends and societal integrity shifts within Indonesia’s demographic segments.

Generational Insights

  • Younger age groups (<40) show greater fluctuation and occasional declines, signaling a need for stronger civic education and anti-corruption awareness among youth.
  • Middle-aged (40–50) and older groups (>60) maintain steady upward trends, suggesting deeper understanding and engagement with integrity-based social values.
  • These generational differences indicate how anti-corruption norms are internalized differently across age groups — a key insight for future civic programs and educational campaigns.

Indonesian Anti-Corruption Behaviors Index by Dimension (2012–2024)

Perception vs Experience Behaviors

This visualization compares the perception and experience dimensions of Indonesia’s Anti-Corruption Behavior Index (IPAK) from 2012 to 2024. It captures how citizens view corruption versus how they actually experience it in their daily interactions.

Key Insights

  • In 2024, both perception and experience indexes slightly declined, signaling a potential challenge in sustaining the progress made over the previous decade.
  • From 2017 to 2023, both dimensions improved significantly, reflecting stronger institutional efforts and growing civic awareness.
  • However, the perception index remains relatively low, suggesting that many Indonesians still perceive corruption as prevalent despite tangible progress in real experiences.
  • Bridging this gap between perception and experience will be crucial for reinforcing long-term trust in anti-corruption governance.
